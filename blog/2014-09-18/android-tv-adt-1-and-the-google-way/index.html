Android TV, ADT-1, and The Google Way

Lorenzo Swank • Sep 18, 2014

Late last week I noticed a box sitting on one of the workspaces in our office. It arrived with no fanfare. I had eagerly been awaiting the Moto 360 promised to Google I/O attendees. The larger box escaped notice — perhaps for several days. I vaguely remember signing up for the Android TV development kit, but there was no confirmation e-mail. My information simply disappeared into the void.

This is typical of the way Google does things. It’s not clear what the process is, and each team probably has their own process. Some teams are better at communicating than others.

The box indicates that it is a “Console”. While I started on Wyse remote consoles, the presence of a controller indicates that the Google team may be thinking of this as a game console. “It looks like an Xbox controller,” was the common refrain.

The device itself looks pretty. It’s flat and black on the top. It also comes with a piece of plastic on the top.

The bottom is a rubberized set of upside-down pyramids. There’s a nice little LED underneath that displays lots of colors.

The actual device experience is clearly meant to be a game console with plenty of set top box features. This is, of course, a pre-release device, so there’s not a lot there yet.

We have a test suite of apps we port over to every new device, just to show that we can do something with it. Since this arrived out of the blue, we haven’t done anything with it yet.

Initial impressions

Android TV’s ADT-1 is the unexpected love child of the Ouya and an Apple TV. It looks like someone flattened out an Apple TV, decided it would get too hot, and put some plastic spacers on the bottom. The operating system is Android, but in a less vanilla flavor than the Ouya used. This is part of Google’s Android L preview, and takes most of the design cues from the new Material Design guidelines Google has pushed this year.

The Sad Truth about Google Hardware

The rumor mill indicates that the remaining stock of ADT-1 units were shipped out after Joe Britt (formerly of Danger) left Google’s Android@Home team. It was originally the Nexus TV, perhaps a reference design such as Google’s Nexus phones. Having the hardware project lead leave does not bode well for the hardware project. Hopefully Google continues to push Android TV, the software side of things. It appears to be part of the Android L push.

As for a Google-made set top box? ADT-1 may quickly be going the way of the Nexus Q.
