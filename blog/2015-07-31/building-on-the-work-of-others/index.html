Free Code Fridays: Building on the Work of Others

Daniel Blakemore • Jul 31, 2015

The best part of working in a thriving, open-source-friendly ecosystem like iOS is that sometimes smart people fix hard problems for you. Working hard to craft a concise, elegant solution to a hard problem is one of the great joys of writing software. One of the lesser joys is meeting deadlines; unfortunately, that's the one that pays the bills.

Sometimes, someone else in the world has already solved your problem, making it easy to avoid writing a "band-aid" solution or, even worse, an unhygienic hack. In other cases, you have a cool solution that you worked hard to make and now you want to share it with others. In both cases, living in an open-source world makes writing software less of a headache.

Over the next few weeks, I will be sharing some of the components, categories, controls, and utilities that we've written to make our lives a little bit easier. As a starter, I've included a fork, a category, and a wrapper class. This will probably be the least exciting installment in this series, but these fit the theme of the opening and the wrapper class in particular has made my life easier.

The Fork: SVSegmentedControl

Late last year, a client of ours hired a designer who had a habit of making designs that were a hodgepodge of controls and concepts from existing apps, all loosely coordinated with a central color scheme. We've all been there. But he wanted to have an animated switch-like segmented control in the navigation bar on one particular set of screens. He'd seen it somewhere and really liked it, but he wanted it to look just so in his flat, iOS 7 color scheme.

I love it when I can exactly match what a designer has in their head. I often start off at www.cocoacontrols.com to avoid duplicating effort, and to see if there was anything that I could use or adapt. There I found SVSegmentedControl, a control from the same mind that graced the world with SVProgressHUD. The problem was, even though it had all sorts of configuration options, none of them affected the gradients or embossed look of pre-iOS 7 designs. I added that functionality. These changes can be found in the CocoaPod SVSegmentedControl@PTEz. I also went ahead and added HeaderDoc comments to all the methods on the control because when I'm developing Option+Click is my friend.

Boom. Everything the designer had ever dreamed of had come true, all in one small refactor. (Well, at least everything having to do with this small switch.) And I had shared the joy of this added functionality with the rest of the world. It was a good day.

The Category: UIAlertController-BetterConstructor

This one might not seem like a big deal, but I find it really shines in combination with the wrapper class that follows.

With the roll out of iOS 8 came some notable deprecations. Like any cutting-edge iOS developer, I immediately wanted to drop using UIAlertView in favor of this new magical class, UIAlertController. However, Apple also introduced a wonderful set of consumer-confidence-shattering bugs that brought them relatively poor adoption rates for their new OS. What this meant for developers is that, unlike with iOS 6, we couldn't cast off our old habits in favor of the glorious new APIs handed down from atop the Cupertino altar. Instead, we had to start coping with the same subtle sadness that Android developers had been coping with for years: API version compatibility checking.

In the case of UIAlertView and UIAlertController, I had two choices:

1) Keep using the deprecated version and deal with warnings and a sinking feeling of self-judged inadequacy.

2) Engineer my way out of the problem.

The first step was to bend UIAlertController to my will. Specifically, this meant crafting a way to use it without having to worry about instances of UIAlertAction or even using -alloc at all.

This was the birth of the BetterConstructor category. In a nutshell, I wanted a class method with an interface that would allow me to specify all the necessary attributes to create an instance of UIAlertController as an alert or a sheet. Ideally, this would allow me to specify all types of blocks (destructive, cancel, default) with as many buttons as I wanted. That's exactly what I did. Now you can get an instance of UIAlertController with your own button titles, button blocks, message, alert title, and style, all with one method call - and without the hassle of having to remember UIAlertAction, or even that it exists.

It's a minor addition, but it allows the next and final item in today's post to be implemented with ease.

The Wrapper Class: PXBlockAlertView

For anyone who has been developing long enough to appreciate the ever increasing presence of blocks in Obj-C code everywhere, the removal of delegates in favor of blocks probably brings a smile to your face. It does to mine. Way back when, there was a category on UIAlertView called AlertViewBlocks written by Jiva DeVoe. This category did away with the annoying UIAlertViewDelegate protocol in favor of some new constructors that provided a way to specify a button title and block to perform that button's action: RIButtonItem. When I first discovered this category, I was in love.

I love blocks and hate delegate overuse so AlertViewBlocks seemed like a perfect solution. However, I don't like messing around with instances of classes that I use once. Things like SVProgressHUD have class methods to do everything you want; you don't have to worry about instantiating them or keeping track of instances. The annoying flaw of AlertViewBlocks was that it required you to instantiate RIButtonItems along with your alert view and provide your blocks and button titles there. In response, I created a version that provides a class method to instantiate and show an alert with all blocks and titles specified as arguments. PXBlockAlertView was born.

Note: technically this Pod’s birth name was PXAlertView but that was taken before I decided to move it out of our private spec repo.

And for a time it was good. Then iOS 8 happened. See above, but I wanted to keep PXBlockAlertView up to date. So I integrated the UIAlertController-BetterConstructor category into it and had it select which class to use based on whether or not the new UIAlertController was available. At this point, this is my de facto method for showing alerts. I almost never need text boxes or destructive buttons so I can get away with the simple case of one- or two-button alerts and I don't have to sweat the details of OS versions. In addition, for simplicity, this class presents UIAlertControllers on the root view controller of the window, so you don't even need to worry about that.
